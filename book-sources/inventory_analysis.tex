\section{Inventory control, analytic results}

In this section we establish a set of formulas by which we can compute several performance measures for the basestock, the $(Q,r)$ and the $(T,S)$ inventory models as functions of the policy parameter(S).
Thus, with these formulas we can avoid simulation to obtain insight into the policy performance.
We will not deal with the $(s,S)$ policy because that is a bit too difficult for these notes.\sidenote{For those interested in a proof of the optimality of $(s,S)$ poliicies and an algorithm to compute the optimal policy parameters see~\citet{foreest23}.}

The period demands $\{D_{i}\}$ are assumed to be iid rvs.
Write
\begin{align*}
  D(i, j) &= \sum_{k=i+1}^{j-1} D_k, &   D[i, j] &= D_{i} + D(i, j,  &   D[i, j] &= D[i, j) + D_{j},
\end{align*}
and likewise notation for $Q(i, j]$.

\newthought{For the basetock} model in discrete time, recall from~\cref{eq:b9} that the start-of-period inventory position is always equal to the order-up-to level $S=s+1$, i.e., $P_{t}=S$, and that the end-of-period $P_t' = S - D_{t}$.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:1}
Under a basestock policy with reorder level~$s$, if $\IP_{1} = S$, then for all periods~$t$,
\begin{equation*}
    D[1, t) = Q(1, t],
\end{equation*}
that is, all demand is either delivered or outstanding.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Using the recursion for the inventory position under the basestock policy repeatedly,
\begin{align*}
    \IP_t
&= \IP_{t-1} -D_{t-1}+ Q_t  \\
&= \IP_{t-2} -D_{t-2}+ Q_{t-1} - D_{t-1} - Q_t  \\
&= \IP_1 - D[1, t) +     Q(1, t].
\end{align*}
The proof is finished by realizing that $\IP_{t} = S$ for all~$t$.
\end{proof}

We can use this lemma to obtain a useful result.
\begin{lemma}\label{lem:2}
Under a basestock policy with reorder level~$s$, if $\IL_{1}=\IP_{1} = S$ and $Q_{t} = 0$ for $t \leq 1$, then for all periods~$t$,
  \begin{equation*}
    \IL_t= S - D[t-L, t).
  \end{equation*}
hence,
\begin{equation*}
  \IL_t'= \IL_{t} - D_{t} = S - D[t-L, t].
\end{equation*}
In words, items not on-hand must be on order.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Using the recursion for the inventory level and the assumptions,
\begin{align*}
  \IL_{t}
&= \IL_{t-1} -D_{t-1}+ Q_{t-L} \\
&= \IL_{t-2}- D_{t-2} +Q_{t-L-1} - D_{t-1} + Q_{t-L}\\
&= \IL_{1}- D[1, t) + Q(1, t-L] \\
&\stackrel 1= S - D[1-L, t),
\end{align*}
where we apply~\cref{lem:1} to $Q(1, t-L]$ in step 1.
\end{proof}

Since the period demand are iid rvs, the rvs $\{D(t-L, t]\}$, while not independent, do have the same distribution.
Necessarily the rvs $\{I_{t}\}$ have the same distribution.
We can associate a rv~$I$ to this distribution, and we call this rv the stochastic inventory level $I(S)$, and likewise for the end-of-period inventory level $I'(S)$; we include the reference to the basestock level~$S$ for ease.
Let us write~$F$ for the cdf common to the rvs $\{D[t-L, t]]$ and let $X\sim F$ be the associated rv.
The cdf~$F$ of~$X$ follows from straightforward convolution\sidenote{We show in code how to do this.}, and from~$F$ we obtain easiy the pmf~$f$ and the survivor function $G=1-F$.


In~\cref{sec:single-item-invent} we express the performance measures in terms of the of-period inventory level.
The ready rate follows directly from the relation $I'=S-X$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:5}
\alpha(S) =  \P{I'\geq 0} = \P{X\leq S} = F(S).
\end{equation}
The two other  service levels can  only be computed numerically.\sidenote{To the extent, I know.}
For the mean inventory level,
\begin{equation*}
\E{I'(S)} = s + 1 - \theta = s + 1 - (L+1)\E D.
\end{equation*}
Noting that $I'^+(S) - I'^-(S) = I'(S)$,
\begin{equation*}
\E{\IL'^+(S)} = \E{I'(S)} + \E{I'^-(S)} = S - \theta + \E{I'^-(S)}.
\end{equation*}
Since $I'^{-}(S) = [-I'(S)]^{+} = [X-S]^{+}$,  we obtain for the average backorder level
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ia22}
\E{\IL'^-(S)} =\sum_{k=0}^\infty (k- S)^+f(k).
\end{equation}
Clearly, this summation runs to $\infty$, which is problematic for numerical purposes. It turns out that it can be rewritten to a simpler form.
\begin{lemma}
\label{lem:3}
The average end-of-period backlog is equal to
\begin{equation*}
   \E{\IL'^-(S)} = \theta - \sum_{j=0}^{s} G(j).
\end{equation*}
Mind, the~$s$ in the RHS is the reorder level $s=S-1$, not~$S$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof} Since $\sum_{j=0}^\infty \1{j< k-r - 1} = k-r -1$, from~\cref{eq:ia22}
\begin{align*}
   \sum_{k=S}^\infty (k-S) f(k)
   &= \sum_{k=S}^\infty\sum_{j=0}^\infty \1{j < k-S}\, f(k)   =
    \sum_{j=0}^\infty \sum_{k=S}^\infty \1{k > j +S}\, f(k)\\
   &= \sum_{j=0}^\infty  G(j+S)
   = \sum_{j=0}^\infty  G(j) - \sum_{j=0}^{s} G(j).
\end{align*}
Because $\sum_{t=0}^\infty G(i) = \theta$ the claim follows.
\end{proof}


With these expressions the average inventory cost because
\begin{equation*}
c(S) =  h \E{I^{+}(S)} +  b \E{I^-(S)} = h \E{I(S)} +  (h+b) \E{I^-(S)}.
\end{equation*}
Minimize the average cost is simple.

GIVES A THRESHOLD POLICY.



\newthought{For the $(s, Q)$ model} we can also find some nice closed form expressions.
There is a nice way to relate the $(Q,r)$ system to a number of basestock systems.
To see this, imagine two animals, a quoll that observes the $(Q,r)$ system, and a bear that only wakes up when the inventory position right at the start of the interval equals~$i$ where $k\in \{r+1, \ldots r+Q\}$ and hibernates otherwise.
Clearly, from the bear's point of view, the system behaves as a basestock system with order-up-to level~$k$.
More generally, we can associate a different bear to each different order-up-to level, so that always exactly one bear is awake, and the rest sleeps.
To combine the statistics of the bears into the statistics as observed by the quoll, we need to know for each~$k$ the average fraction of periods  the inventory position $\IP = k$. More specifically, the quoll needs to know\sidenote{We assume that the limit over all sample paths $\{\IP_{t}\}$ exists almost surely, so that we can invoke the strong law of large numbers.}
\begin{equation*}
\pi_{k} = \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T}\1{\IP_t = k},
\end{equation*}
because with this, the quoll can retrieve the ready rate $\sum_{k=r+1}^{Q+r} \pi_k \alpha(k)$ from the ready rates $\alpha(k)$ of the bears.



\begin{theorem}
The  probabilities $\pi_{k} = \P{\IP = k}$, $k=r+1, \ldots, r+Q$,  under the $(Q,r)$ are all equal, that is, $\pi_k = 1/Q$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
For ease take $Q=3, r=0$, so that the inventory position $\IP$ cycles between the levels $1, 2$ and~$3$.\sidenote{The proof is the same for general~$Q$ and~$r$,  but needs a bit more notation.}
We can use level crossing arguments as follows.
Write $p_{i} = \P{D\in \{i, 3+i, 6+i, \ldots\}}$ for the probability that the period demand is a multiple of~$3$ offset by~$i$, $i=0, 1, 2$.
Suppose that, at the start of period~$t$, the position $\IP_{t} = 3$.
Since we order in multiples of $Q=3$, the probability $\P{\IP_{t+1} =3|\IP_{t}=3} = p_{0}$, i.e., equal to the probability that $D_{t}$ is a multiple of~$3$.
Likewise, $\P{\IP_{t+1} = 2|\IP_t=3} = p_{1}$, and $\P{\IP_{t+1} = 1|\IP_t=3} = p_{2}$.
However, again because we order in multiples of~$Q$, $\P{\IP_{t+1} = 1|\IP_t=2} = p_{1}$, and similarly for the other possibities.
Therefore, we can use level-crossing to conclude that on the long run, $(p_{1}+p_2)\pi_{3} = p_2\pi_{2} + p_1 \pi_{1}$. But, because of the $(Q,r)$ ordering rule, we can cyclically change $\pi_{1}, \pi_{2}$ and $\pi_{3}$ is this equality. The only  solution that is compatible with this is that $\pi_{i} = 1/3$ for all~$i$.
\end{proof}

With the above theorem, the ready rate for the $(Q,r)$ model can be computed with the formula
\begin{align}
   \alpha(Q,r) = \frac1Q \sum_{k=r}^{r+Q-1} \alpha(k), % = \frac1Q \sum_{k=s}^{s+Q-1} F(k+1)
\end{align}
where $\alpha(k)$ is the ready rate  of the basestock model with reorder level~$k$. Interestingly, the ready rate of the $(Q,r)$ policy can be expressed in terms of the
average backlog of the basestock model.

\begin{lemma}
The ready rate of the $(Q,r)$ model is equal to
\begin{align}
   \alpha(Q,r) = 1 - \frac1Q(\E{I^{-}(r-1)} - \E{I^{-}(r+Q-1)}).
\end{align}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
From~\cref{eq:5}, and the fact that $G(k) = 1- F(k)$,
\begin{align*}
\sum_{k=r+1}^{r+Q} \alpha(k)
  &=  \sum_{k=r+1}^{r+Q} F(k)
  =  \sum_{k=0}^{r+Q} F(k)  -  \sum_{k=0}^{r} F(k) \\
  &= Q - \sum_{k=0}^{r+Q} G(k)  +  \sum_{k=0}^{r} G(k).
%  &= Q + \E{I^{-}(r+Q)} -  \E{I^{-}(r)}.
\end{align*}
Dividing by~$Q$ and using~\cref{lem:3} gives the result.
\end{proof}

For the average inventory level  we find
\begin{align*}
   \E{I'(Q,r)}
   &= \frac1Q\sum_{t=r+1}^{r+Q} \E{I'(i)}
     = \frac1Q\sum_{t=r+1}^{r+Q} (i - \theta) \\
   &= \frac{Q+1}2 + r - \theta,
\end{align*}
and for the expected number of back-orders
\begin{equation*}
   \E{I'^-(Q,r)} = \frac1Q \sum_{k=r+1}^{r+Q} \E{I'^-(k)},
\end{equation*}
From this, $\E{I'^{+}(Q,r)} = \E{I'(Q,r)} + \E{I'^{-}(Q,r)}$.
Finally, with~$h$ and~$b$ the holding and backorder cost per item per period,  the total average cost becomes
\begin{equation*}
  K\frac{\E D}{Q} + h \E{I'(Q,r)} +  (b+h) \E{I'^-(Q,r)}.
\end{equation*}
With regard to the first term, note that $\E D$ is average demand per period, so $\E D / Q$ is the order frequency per period.


It remains to find good values for~$r$ and~$Q$.
One way would be to carry out a full grid search over a (reasonable) set of pairs of~$r$ and~$Q$.
Another simple heuristic is to take $Q=\sqrt{2\E{D}K/h}$, i.e., the EOQ value, and then search for~$r$ such that the total average cost is minimal, or such that some service level criterion is met.
and take for~$r$ the optimal basestock level, as if $Q=1$.


For the $(s,S)$ model we can take~$s$ to be the same as for the $(s,Q)$ model, and then set $S=s+Q$.

\begin{exercise}
  Here is a subtle problem, that you should think about, and \emph{memorize}. In these notes so far, we have been concerned with \emph{periodic-review} systems: at the end of period~$i$, we check the inventory $I_t$. Hence, if $I_t\geq 0$, all demand must have been satisfied.

  In continuous-review systems,  the notation $I_t$ corresponds to the inventory level as perceived by the~$i$th customer (observe that this is very different from the meaning of the $I_t$ for period-review systems).

  Why is the ready rate $F(S)$ (and not $F(S)=F(s+1)$ as in \cref{eq:13}) for continuous-review systems?
  \begin{solution}
    In continuous time, when $I_t=0$, the~$i$th demand `sees' an empty inventory. Hence, the~$i$th demand cannot be met from on-hand stock when $I_t=0$. Thus, only when $I_t>0$, this demand can be served. Therefore,
\begin{align*}
   \alpha(s) &= \P{\IL_t > 0} \\
   &= \P{r+1 - D[i-L, i] > 0} \\
   &= \P{D[i-L, i] <  r+1} \\
   &= \P{D[i-L, i] \leq  r} \\
   & = F(r).
\end{align*}
  \end{solution}
\end{exercise}
